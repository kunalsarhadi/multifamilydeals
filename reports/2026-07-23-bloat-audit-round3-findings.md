**Date:** July 23, 2026 · **Scope:** all 13 non-blog pages, every section judged top-to-bottom · **Nothing executed — review only.**
Test applied to every block: *"Would deleting this make the page convert worse?"* If not clearly yes → flagged.

---

## Executive Verdict

**The site does not have a wrong-content problem — it has a repetition problem.** Nearly every section was built for a valid psychological reason; the bloat comes from the same facts and the same proof strips appearing 3–5 times per visit. The reader isn't confused because any one section is bad — they're confused because the site keeps re-proving points they already accepted, which (a) buries the product and the new information, and (b) trains readers to skim gold callout boxes — which then costs attention on the CTAs styled the same way.

**Counts that make the point:**
- why-alberta.html states **~4% vacancy 5 times** and **$0 PST 5 times** on one page
- **5% / 50-yr** appears on all 13 pages, usually 2–3× each
- The appraisal claim has **~10 surfaces** site-wide plus a nav link on every page — up to 3 exposures per page
- index and inventory show the **identical two review quotes** (Chad F. / Nestor G.) — making "160+ reviews" look like 2
- The homepage "Three Tax Advantages" section restates comparison-grid rows the reader finished 10 seconds earlier — the clearest single cut on the site
- FAQ gives **two different answers to "how much money do I need"** ($90–105K vs $102–108K liquid for a 6-plex) in two different groups

**Every nav tab and page earns its place.** Nothing at the tab level should be removed. Calculator is the model page (zero cuts — the visitor persuades themselves). Contact and buying-process are near-clean. The bloat is concentrated in: index (2 sections), inventory (sequencing + strip stack), why-alberta (1 whole section + repetition), about (2 sections), ontario (1 section), FAQ (1 group), and cross-page strip repetition.

## WHAT'S MISSING (merged from both audits; excludes known backlog: photos, outcome data, price bands, GHL calculator form)

1. **One worked deal, told as a story.** Stats everywhere, narrative nowhere. A single static "anatomy of one deal" ($2.25M 8-plex → $112K deposit → rents → PM fee → net cash flow → year-2 equity, at the canonical 4%, labelled illustrative) plus optionally one investor arc. Desire is the weakest stage of every page's sequence; stories fix that. Homes: index (between timeline and reviews), ontario (after proof band).
2. **"Why buy through us instead of going direct?"** The site sells Edmonton + MLI Select so well that a persuaded reader could take the thesis to any realtor or straight to a builder. Nothing anywhere names what MFD adds (allocation access, pre-negotiated incentives, pre-vetted pro-formas, scale track record). One tight section on index + ontario closes the leak.
3. **"How we get paid."** Silence on compensation reads as the catch to this persona. One honest line/FAQ ("we're compensated as the brokerage on the transaction; you pay us nothing directly" — owner to confirm exact wording) converts suspicion into trust.
4. **A consolidated "what can go wrong" block.** Risk answers exist but are scattered (recourse in FAQ, CMHC-declines on inventory, appraisal-below in FAQ). One volunteered risk section — rate at renewal, slower lease-up, builder delay, market cyclicality — is the strongest possible differentiation from get-rich-quick competitors, and it pre-empts the spouse/accountant veto (the real second decision-maker).
5. **What the discovery call actually is** — 3 lines near primary CTAs: what happens, what we ask, what you leave with, "no pitch." Lowers the booking threshold for a time-protective persona.
6. **A capital→building-size router.** Nothing maps "I have ~$100K" → "you're an 8-plex buyer — these three." Navigation, not price disclosure. Fits inventory or as calculator output.
7. **Post-submit expectations on contact** ("confirmation text; Kunal reviews within 1 business day") + **exit-intent on contact** (the page where abandonment costs most; feeds the documented did-not-submit retargeting segment).
8. **The exit/hold story.** Who buys an 8-plex in year 6? How liquid is it? Terminal liquidity is standard diligence for a $100K+ HELOC decision; the site never touches it.
9. **A dated urgency mechanism.** All scarcity is stock-count. Nothing gives a *now* reason: next release date, completion-window pricing, rate-lock windows. One owner-verifiable dated line would arm every Book CTA.
10. **Founder voice before photos arrive.** One signed first-person paragraph ("why we only sell what we'd buy") on index or contact bridges the human gap until photography exists. Also: Ontario page's remote-closing claim rests on a single Toronto quote — one more remote-buyer proof point would harden the page's central promise.
